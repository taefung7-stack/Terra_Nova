The Origin of the Universe
The Big Bang theory says the universe began about 13.8 billion years ago from a tiny, hot point. Proof comes from the cosmic microwave background, a faint radiation found across all of space. Astronomers also see galaxies moving apart, which means the universe is still expanding today.

Supply, Demand, and Markets
Markets run on the balance of supply and demand. When buyers want more than sellers can offer, prices rise. When supply is greater than demand, prices fall. This system shares goods without central planning, though markets can still fail when information is unequal.

Exponential growth means values increase faster and faster over time. Starting small, numbers can double again and again until they become huge. Bacteria, compound interest, and viral spread all follow this pattern. Small beginnings can reach surprising sizes very quickly.

The Strange World of Quantum Mechanics
Quantum mechanics describes matter and energy at the smallest scales. At this level, particles like electrons act as both waves and particles, depending on how we observe them. This wave-particle duality defies common sense, yet it has been confirmed in countless experiments. It also forms the basis of modern technology, from semiconductors to medical imaging.

Central Banks and Monetary Policy
Central banks shape the economy by setting interest rates and controlling the money supply. When inflation is high, they raise rates to cool demand. When growth is weak, they lower rates to encourage spending. But monetary policy alone cannot fix every issue, and a wrong move can trigger recession or runaway inflation.

The Foundation of Calculus
Calculus is the mathematics of change and accumulation. Derivatives measure how fast something changes at a single moment. Integrals add up values over a whole range, helping us find total distance or area. The fundamental theorem of calculus shows that these two ideas are actually inverse operations.

Literary Criticism
Literary criticism examines how texts create meaning. Formalist critics focus on the structure and language of the text itself. Feminist critics study gender and power within stories. Marxist critics look at class and economic forces. Each view reveals something that the others might miss.

창발적 속성
The Mystery of Emergence
Emergence is when a complex system shows properties its parts do not have alone. A single water molecule is not "wet," yet billions together feel unmistakably wet. The same may be true of consciousness: no single neuron can think, yet billions together produce the mind. This idea challenges simple cause-and-effect thinking and pushes us to study systems as whole units.

Democracy and the Protection of Liberty
Democracy is often described as majority rule, but this misses a deeper principle. Without protections for minorities, pure majority rule can become what James Madison called "the tyranny of the majority." True democracy guarantees rights such as free speech and fair trial that no passing majority can overturn. Separation of powers also prevents any single group from taking total control.

Abstract Mathematical Structures
Abstract algebra studies math at its deepest level. A group, for instance, is a set with an operation that follows four rules: closure, associativity, identity, and inverses. Integers under addition form a group, but so do the rotations of a molecule. Though they look different, they share the same structure, and one proof can apply to both.

Deconstruction and Textual Meaning
Deconstruction, linked to the philosopher Jacques Derrida, challenges the idea that texts have fixed meaning. Derrida argued that every word gains meaning only by contrast with what it is not, so meaning is never fully settled. Each reader also brings a unique history that shapes interpretation. Texts may even contradict their own claims, revealing hidden tensions the author never intended.
